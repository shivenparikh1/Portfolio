Executive Summary

Why This Process Matters

This project converts MSWIPE's logistics SOP and warehouse observation notes into a structured business process case study. The focus is on two linked warehouse flows: fresh inward of imported MPOS devices and reverse inward of refurbished, faulty, or non-working devices received from field or merchant-facing channels.

Warehouse inward is important because it connects physical stock, supplier documentation, ERP records, and financial posting. A device is not truly usable for operations just because it has arrived at the warehouse. It must be verified, scanned, matched with uploaded records, recorded in the internal system, moved through quality checks, and handed off correctly to the next department.

Reverse inward is equally important because returned devices need clear traceability. The warehouse team must understand where the device came from, whether the device is free for inward movement, what condition it is in, whether quality or repair action is required, and how the ERP and flat-file records should reflect the actual movement.

Through this case study, I studied how a structured SOP reduces operational risk by linking documents, physical device verification, ERP stock transfers, QC labels, RMA movement, and department communication into one controlled workflow.

Company Context

MSWIPE Logistics Environment

MSWIPE operates in a business environment where physical devices and related materials must be carefully tracked across receiving, storage, dispatch, return, testing, repair, and final handling. The logistics and warehouse function supports this movement by maintaining control over device records, stock status, and departmental handoffs.

The supply chain department's role includes coordinating device movement, maintaining warehouse documentation, supporting inventory accuracy, connecting warehouse activity with ERP and flat-file records, and sharing verified details with finance, accounting, quality, operations, and repair teams.

Fresh Inward Process

Import Device Receiving Flow

The SOP describes fresh inward as the process for imported MPOS devices received at the warehouse after supplier documentation and customs-related movement are completed.

1

Vendor Shipment Documents Are Shared

When goods are handed over to the shipping agent, the supplier shares the invoice copy, packing list, and device details with the finance team. These documents become the starting reference for receiving and system preparation.

2

Finance Uploads Device Details In ERP

The finance team uploads device details through a bulk upload in the ERP. This creates an expected system record before warehouse scanning begins.

3

Shipment Reaches The Warehouse

Once the devices are received by the logistics team at the warehouse, the warehouse team begins the inward process by identifying the shipment and preparing for physical scanning.

4

Physical Scanning Captures Device IDs

Each device is physically scanned by the logistics team. The ERP captures the device ID and cross-verifies the scanned data against records uploaded earlier by finance.

5

Matched Entries Proceed

The system permits only matched entries to proceed for scanning. This prevents unrecognized or mismatched device records from moving forward without review.

6

Flat File Is Updated

After successful scanning, device data is updated in the flat file. This supports traceability between ERP records and operational tracking files.

7

Discrepancies Are Reported

If short or excess inventory is found, the logistics team reports the discrepancy to finance. Finance coordinates with the OEM or supplier to resolve the issue.

8

Product Testing Sample Is Sent

After fresh inward, one unit is sent to the product team for functionality testing. The ERP transfer to the testing team and return to store is handled like an inter-batch stock transfer.

9

Devices Move To QC

Once the tested device is received back by the store, devices are moved to the quality department. The store manager performs the ERP stock transfer to the QC department grid.

10

QC Status Determines Next Movement

QC checks devices against quality parameters such as firmware, slot working, application upload, ROM check, and entry-level screen. Fresh inward devices that pass QC receive green labels and move back to store for allocation and dispatch.

Reverse Inward Process

Refurbished, Faulty, And Non-Working Device Flow

The SOP describes reverse inward as refurbished or non-working devices received from field staff executives and direct merchant channels. These devices require status review before they can be moved into store, QC, repair, or available stock.

1

Returned Device Is Received At Store Location

Refurbished and non-working devices received at the store location are recorded in both the Excel-based inward register and the ERP system. The ERP entry follows the stock transfer entry process.

2

Inward Register Captures Return Details

The inward register captures device information, consignee details, FSE details, date of receipt, and courier details if applicable. A summary of device name and box number is shared with the store team.

3

Flat-File Status Is Checked

The register includes status categories such as installation completed, pending for installation, pending for activation, pending for deinstallation, and free devices. This status check controls whether the device can move inward.

4

Non-Free Devices Require Closure

For statuses other than free device, the store team coordinates with the respective FSE or consignee to close tickets and enable the status change to free device.

5

Device Moves From FSE/Consignee To Inward Location

Once the device is free and the ERP entry is completed, device movement is recorded from FSE or consignee to the inward location in ERP. The flat file is updated as well.

6

Device Moves From Inward To Store

From the inward location, devices are moved to the store or warehouse location. The store team verifies received devices against the inward register.

7

Store Sends Device To QC

The store team checks device condition and sends the device to the quality department. The ERP movement from store to QC follows the stock transfer process, and the flat-file location shifts from FSE to store.

8

QC Determines Okay Or Repair Required

If the device is quality done and found okay, it returns to the store team. If it does not meet quality requirements, it is sent to the RMA team for repair.

9

RMA Repairs And Returns To QC

The RMA team checks device condition and replaces parts if needed. The device is then returned to QC through ERP transfer for another quality check.

10

Final Status Supports Allocation Or Further Action

Devices that pass QC can move back to store for allocation and dispatch. Devices that need repair, replacement, or investigation remain controlled through RMA, supplier coordination, or status-specific handling.

Reverse Inward Detail

Status Assignment And Final Handling

Free Device

Device status that allows transfer from FSE or consignee to inward location and then to store. This status is a key gate in the reverse inward process.

Pending Installation / Activation / Deinstallation

Statuses that require coordination before inward movement can be completed. The store team coordinates with the relevant field or consignee contact for ticket closure.

QC Passed

Device has completed quality checks and can return to store for allocation and dispatch. Refurbished devices receive a yellow QC label after QC completion.

Repair Required

Device does not meet QC requirements and is transferred to the repairs and maintenance team. Devices received from RMA after repair are marked with an orange label after QC.

Supplier Replacement Review

If a part is irreplaceable, the repair team informs finance, and finance coordinates with the supplier for device replacement.

Merchant Damage Exception

When merchant-reported damage is handled through a helpdesk ticket, field inspection, repair cost communication, payment confirmation, and standard inward flow.

Document Flow

How Documents Support Stock Movement

Fresh Inward Document Flow

  1. Purchase order prepared and approved
  2. Supplier invoice, packing list, and device details shared
  3. Finance uploads device details in ERP
  4. Warehouse scans devices and captures device IDs
  5. ERP validates scanned devices against uploaded data
  6. Flat file is updated after successful scanning
  7. Discrepancy notes are shared with finance, if required
  8. Verified records support accounting and payment review

Reverse Inward Document Flow

  1. Returned device details are entered in inward register
  2. Device, consignee, FSE, date, and courier details are captured
  3. Flat-file device status is reviewed
  4. Ticket closure is coordinated for non-free statuses
  5. ERP stock transfer moves device to inward location
  6. Device moves from inward to store and then QC
  7. QC/RMA movement is recorded through ERP stock transfer
  8. Final status supports allocation, repair, or replacement handling

Operational Issues

Common Issues The Process Controls Against

Fresh Inward Issues

  • Invoice, packing list, or device detail mismatch
  • Short inventory received against expected quantity
  • Excess inventory received beyond expected records
  • Device ID not matching finance-uploaded ERP records
  • Delayed flat-file update after scanning
  • QC-failed devices accidentally treated as available stock
  • Incomplete discrepancy communication to finance

Reverse Inward Issues

  • Returned device status not marked as free device
  • Ticket closure pending before inward movement
  • Missing inward register details
  • Device condition not clearly captured before QC
  • Returned items mixed with QC-passed stock
  • Device movement not updated in ERP or flat file
  • Repair-required devices not handed off cleanly to RMA

Improvement Ideas

Possible Process Improvements

Based on the process map, these are possible process improvement ideas that could support accuracy and visibility.

Standard Receiving Checklist

A simple checklist for invoice, packing list, device scan, discrepancy, flat-file update, and ERP status completion.

Reverse Inward Register Template

A structured register with required fields for source, reason, device ID, status, QC result, and final handling.

Discrepancy Log

A single log for short inventory, excess inventory, unmatched device IDs, and unresolved supplier coordination.

Status Labels For Returns

Clear visual labels for pending QC, QC passed, RMA required, repaired, excess hold, and replacement review.

Processing Time Tracking

Timestamp each stage to identify bottlenecks in scanning, ticket closure, QC, RMA, and ERP update.

Pending Item Dashboard

A dashboard that shows devices pending verification, QC, RMA, finance clarification, or final stock movement.

Digital Document Checklist

A shared checklist that confirms document readiness before accounting handoff or supplier escalation.

Separate Return Zones

Physically separate damaged, pending, QC-passed, and usable return stock to avoid accidental mixing.

What I Learned

Reflection From The Observation

I learned that warehouse operations require both physical accuracy and system accuracy. A device must be present in the warehouse, but it also needs the correct ERP status, flat-file update, location, and department movement record.

I learned that invoices, packing lists, purchase entries, ERP updates, quality checks, and accounting handoffs are all connected. If one step is incomplete, it can affect inventory visibility, finance review, dispatch planning, and operational confidence.

I learned that reverse inward can be just as important as fresh inward because returned devices need clear status and traceability. A returned device should not simply be placed back into stock without checking whether it is free, verified, quality cleared, repaired, or still pending action.

I learned that a small mismatch can affect multiple teams, including warehouse, finance, accounting, quality, RMA, operations, and field teams. Process mapping helped me understand how each department depends on accurate handoffs from the previous stage.

I also learned that SOP documentation is most useful when it can be converted into practical flowcharts, registers, KPIs, and dashboards that make the workflow easier to understand and monitor.
